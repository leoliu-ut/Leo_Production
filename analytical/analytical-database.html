    Database Application

    Areas of Interest: We examined a lot of areas about cancer. We found three most intriguing ones - the relationship between cancer rate and age group, region and insurance status.

    Datasets :
    Population Information dataset.*
    The National Health Interview Survey dataset contains answers from an annual (year of 2012) cross-sectional survey designed by the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS) and administered by the U.S. Census Bureau. These surveys track health status, health care access, and progress toward achieving national health objectives. The three survey information files are about the overall families’, individual adults’, and individual childrens’ health. The data explores the health and well-being of families in much detail; there are hundreds of unique attributes.

    Cancer Stats dataset.*
    The United States Cancer Statistics (USCS) explores cancer details grouped by cancer by area, cancer by age, and cancer by site. The information is age-adjusted and have crude rate estimations. The information is age-adjusted because the occurrences of many cancers increase with age and death by cancer chances increase with age. This way, the comparing areas with different population age distribution is more accurate.

    Approach:
    Data Modeling:
    Through our SQL transforms, we made many of our modeled tables and had to perform a cast as string on our age field because it contained age ranges as well as singular age numbers.
    SQL :
    We practiced more than 100 SQL queries.
    Dataflow Programming:
    Through PTransforms, we cast age into age groups, sorted race groups, and cast states into regions.
    Two datasets we found have different measures to record age. The population dataset contains specific years, while the cancer stats one only contains age groups. Also, we fixed some issues with question marks, such as "9-5?" and sorted them in order. In the population dataset, Asians are lists as their nationalities such as Chinese, Indian etc, while in the cancer stats one, all Asians are in the same group. Similarly, population dataset contains all specific info about each entry's state origin, while the cancer stats one only contains region such as West, South, Midwest and Northeast.

    What we learned:
    1. The relationship between cancer rate and insurance status
    We found out that the majority of population diagnosed with cancer have insurance covered. This is exciting news!

    What we learned:
    2. The relationship between cancer rate and region
    We found out that people in Midwest are more likely to be diagnosed with cancer at a rate of 0.37. The rate for Northeast is 0.34, South is 0.30, and West is 0.29.

    What we learned:
    3. The relationship between cancer rate and age
    We found out that the majority of population diagnosed with cancer are elder citizens, which echoes with our common sense. 1.79% of people aged 85+ are likely to have cancer, while less than 0.05% for other age groups.
