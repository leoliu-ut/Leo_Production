    Database Application

    Areas of Interest:
    We examined a lot of areas about cancer. We found 3 most intriguing ones - the relationship between cancer rate and age group, region, and insurance status correspondingly.

    What we learned:
    1. The relationship between cancer rate and insurance status
    We found out that the majority of the population diagnosed with cancer have insurance covered. This is exciting news!

    What we learned:
    2. The relationship between cancer rate and region
    We found out that people in Midwest are more likely to be diagnosed with cancer at a rate of 0.37. The rate for Northeast is 0.34, South is 0.30, and West is 0.29.

    What we learned:
    3. The relationship between cancer rate and age
    We found out that the majority of the population diagnosed with cancer are elder citizens, which echoes with our common sense. 1.79% of people aged 85+ are likely to have cancer, while less than 0.05% for other age groups.

    Datasets we used :
    Population Information dataset.*
    The United States Cancer Statistics (USCS) explores cancer details grouped by cancer by area, cancer by age, and cancer by site. The information is age-adjusted and has crude rate estimations. The information is age-adjusted because the occurrences of many cancers increase with age and death by cancer chances increase with age. This way, comparing areas with different population age distributions is more accurate.

    Cancer Stats dataset.*
    The United States Cancer Statistics (USCS) explores cancer details grouped by cancer by area, cancer by age, and cancer by site. The information is age-adjusted and has crude rate estimations. The information is age-adjusted because the occurrences of many cancers increase with age and death by cancer chances increase with age. This way, comparing areas with different population age distributions is more accurate.

    Approach:
    SQL :
    I practiced more than 100 SQL queries.

    Data Modeling:
    Through SQL transforms, I made several modeled tables. I performed a "cast as string" on the age field because it contained age ranges as well as singular age numbers. Through PTransforms, we cast age into age groups, sorted race groups, and cast states into regions.

    I realized that the two datasets we found have different measures to record age. The population dataset contains specific years, while the cancer stats one only contains age groups.

    In the population dataset, Asians are listed as their nationalities such as Chinese, Indian, etc, while in the cancer stats one, all Asians are in the same group. Similarly, the population dataset contains all specific info about each entry's state origin, while the cancer stats one only contains regions such as West, South, Midwest, and Northeast. So together with my teammate, I modeled data to maintain consistency.
